The Jacobian matrix is essentially the derivative of a vector with respect to another vector. To capture all the partial derivatives of this you need a two dimensional structure, hence the Jacobian matrix. First consider a scalar to scalar function $y = f(x)$ with $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$. The derivative of $f$ is simply a $1 \times 1$ matrix, or scalar, $dy / dx$. If we then take $f$ to be a vector-valued function of one variable, i.e. $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n$ so $x \in \mathbb{R}$ and $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$, then applying $f$ gives you an $n$-dimensional vector $f(x) = (f_1(x), f_2(x), \ldots, f_n(x))$. The derivative is then $f'(x) = (f_1'(x), f_2'(x), \ldots, f_n'(x))$, which is an $n$-dimensional vector of derivatives. Next, consider real-values functions of $m > 1$ variables, i.e. $f: \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$. Then $f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m)$ and its derivative is the $m$-dimensional vector $$ \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}_m} \right) $$ If we then take $f$ to be a vector-valued function of $m > 1$ variables, i.e. $f: \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $n > 1$, we have a function of the form: $$ \begin{align} \mathbf{y} & = f(\mathbf{x}) \\ & = f(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m) \\ & = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(\mathbf{x}) \\ f_2(\mathbf{x}) \\ \vdots \\ f_n(\mathbf{x}) \end{bmatrix} \\ & = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m) \\ f_2(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m) \\ \vdots \\ f_n(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_m)) \end{bmatrix} \end{align} $$ with $f$ having $n$ component or coordinate functions $f_1$ through $f_n$. The Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}_f$ is then the $\mathsf{n} \times \mathsf{m}$ matrix of partial derivatives of each component function $f_i$ of $f$ with respect to the values $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m$, i.e. $$ \mathbf{J}_f = \frac{df}{d\mathbf{x}} = [\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_1, \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_2, \ldots, \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} f_n]^{\mathsf{T}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial \mathbf{x}_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial \mathbf{x}_m}\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_n}{\partial \mathbf{x}_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_n}{\partial \mathbf{x}_m} \end{bmatrix} $$ where $f$ and $\mathbf{y}$ are interchangeable. Any $f$ of this form can be considered to be implementing a change of coordinate planes, e.g. taking something in the $m$-dimensional plane where $\mathbf{x}$ lies and mapping it to the $n$-dimensional plane where $\mathbf{y}$ resides.
